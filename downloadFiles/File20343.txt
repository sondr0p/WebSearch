Learn why federal student loans are generally preferable to private loans, and how to complete the Free Application for Federal Student Aid, or FAFSA. </p>

       <h2 id="item-213687">Federal Student Financial Aid and the FAFSA </h2> 
      </header> 
      <h3>What help is available?</h3>
      <p>You can find <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/grants-scholarships">grants and scholarships</a>, <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/loans">student&nbsp;loans</a>, and <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/work-study">work-study programs</a> through <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/about">Federal Student Aid (FSA)</a> to help pay for college or career school.</p>
      <h3>Am I eligible?</h3>
      <p><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/eligibility/basic-criteria">Eligibility requirements for federal student aid</a> include:</p>
      <ul>
       <li><p>Financial need</p></li>
       <li><p>Being a U.S. citizen or an <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/eligibility/non-us-citizens">eligible noncitizen</a></p></li>
       <li><p>Being in good standing on any federal student loans you may have</p></li>
       <li><p>Being in or accepted for an eligible degree or certificate program</p></li>
       <li><p>Maintaining adequate academic progress</p></li>
      </ul>
      <p>Note: This is not a complete list. Review <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/eligibility/basic-criteria">all eligibility requirements</a>.</p>
      <h3>How do I apply?</h3>
      <ol>
       <li><p>Know the deadlines for submitting a <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/fafsa">Free Application for Federal Student Aid (FAFSA)</a>. The federal deadline for the 2018–19 school year is June 30, 2019.&nbsp;Many states and colleges use the FAFSA for their financial aid programs. <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/fafsa#deadlines">Those deadlines vary</a>.</p></li>
       <li><p><a href="https://fsaid.ed.gov/npas/index.htm">Create an FSA ID</a><a href="https://fsaid.ed.gov/npas/index.htm"> </a>account if you’re going to submit your FAFSA online or track its status online. If you’re going to submit a paper FAFSA by mail and won’t be tracking its status, you won’t need an FSA ID.</p></li>
       <li><p><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/fafsa">Complete and submit the FAFSA</a>.</p></li>
       <li><p>Know what happens <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/fafsa/next-steps">after you submit the FAFSA</a>. This includes:</p>
        <ol>
         <li><p>Learning how to correct or update information on it.</p></li>
         <li><p>Finding out how and when you’ll get your aid.</p></li>
        </ol></li>
      </ol>
      <h3>How do I check the status of an application?</h3>
      <p>You can check the status of your FAFSA:</p>
      <ul>
       <li><p>Right after submitting it online</p></li>
       <li><p>Seven to ten days after mailing a paper FAFSA</p></li>
      </ul>
      <p>You can check by:</p>
      <ul>
       <li><p>Going to <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/fafsa">fafsa.gov</a> and logging in. (You must have an <a href="https://fsaid.ed.gov/npas/index.htm">FSA ID</a>.)</p></li>
       <li><p>Contacting the <a href="https://studentaidhelp.ed.gov/app/home/site/fafsa">Federal Student Aid Information Center</a>.</p></li>
      </ul>
      <h3>How do I complain?</h3>
      <ul>
       <li><p>File a complaint or share feedback online through the <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/contact/feedback">FSA Feedback System</a>.</p></li>

For more help with disputes, contact the <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/repay-loans/disputes/prepare#how-do-i-contact">Federal Student Aid Ombudsman Group</a><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/contact/feedback">.</a></p></li>

      <h3>Who do I contact for extra help?</h3>

       <h2 id="item-206091">Get a Student Loan</h2> 
      </header> 
      <h3>Types of Student Loans</h3>
      <p>When you are <a href="https://www.usa.gov/higher-education#item-211286">exploring ways to pay for college, career, or technical schools</a>, you may consider taking out a student loan—money you borrow to help you cover your education expenses and that you must pay back with interest. &nbsp;&nbsp;</p>
      <p>Student loans originate from the federal government (called “federal student loans”) or from private sources, such as a bank, credit union, state agency, or school. Learn the <a href="http://www.consumerfinance.gov/askcfpb/545/what-are-main-differences-between-federal-student-loans-and-private-student-loans.html">differences between federal and private loans</a> before considering a loan.</p>
      <h4>Federal Student Loans</h4>
      <p>If you need to borrow money to pay for college or career school, start with the more affordable federal student loan:</p>
      <p><strong>Types of Federal Student Loan Programs -&nbsp;</strong>The William D. Ford Federal Direct Loan (Direct Loan) Program offers four types of Direct Loans: &nbsp; &nbsp;</p>
      <ul>
       <li><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/loans/subsidized-unsubsidized">Direct Subsidized Loans</a> are made to eligible undergraduate students based on financial need.</li>
       <li><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/loans/subsidized-unsubsidized">Direct Unsubsidized Loans</a> are made to eligible undergraduate, graduate, and professional students, and are not based on financial need.</li>
       <li><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/loans/plus">Direct PLUS Loans</a> are made to graduate or professional students and parents of dependent undergraduate students.</li>
       <li><a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/repay-loans/consolidation">Direct Consolidation Loans</a> allow you to combine all of your eligible federal student loans into a single loan with a single loan servicer.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>The <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/types/loans/perkins">Federal Perkins Loan Program</a> is administered through the schools, and awarded to undergraduates and graduate students with exceptional financial need. If you are eligible, you should take this loan first.</p>
      <p><strong>Eligibility</strong> - You must be enrolled at a school that participates in the school loan program, and <a href="https://studentaid.ed.gov/sa/eligibility">meet the general eligibility requirements</a>.</p>
      <p><strong>How to apply</strong> - Complete the <a href="https://www.usa.gov/financial-aid#item-35757">Free Application for Federal Student Aid</a> or FAFSA. Watch this video to learn more about what happens after <a href="https://www.usa.gov/financial-aid#item-213011">submitting</a> your FAFSA.</p>
      <h4>Private Student Loans</h4>
      <p>Before taking a private loan, make sure you need it. These loans generally are not as affordable as federal student loans, and offer little repayment flexibility. Read these <a href="http://www.consumerfinance.gov/paying-for-college/choose-a-student-loan/#o3">tips before getting a &nbsp;private loan</a>.</p>
      <p>Learn more about <a href="https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/1028-student-loans">student loans, and how to identify deceptive private loan practices</a>.</p> 
     </article>
