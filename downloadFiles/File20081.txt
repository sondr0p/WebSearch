       <h2 id="item-37317">American Money</h2> 
      </header> 
      <p>The United States issues paper currency and coins to pay for purchases, taxes, and debts.</p>
      <h3>Paper Money</h3>
      <p>American paper currency is issued in several <a href="http://www.moneyfactory.gov/uscurrency.html">denominations</a>: $1, $2, $5, $10, $20, $50, and $100. The Bureau of Engraving and Printing manufactures paper money. It also <a href="https://www.moneyfactory.gov/hmimdesigning.html">redesigns money</a>, with new appearances and&nbsp;enhanced <a href="https://www.uscurrency.gov/denominations">security features</a>&nbsp;to prevent counterfeiting. You can <a href="http://www.moneyfactorystore.gov/">purchase commemorative or bulk versions of American currency</a> through the Bureau's Money Store.</p>
      <p>The United States no longer issues bills in larger denominations, such as $500,&nbsp;$1,000, $5,000, and $10,000&nbsp;bills. However, they are still legal tender and&nbsp;may still be in circulation.&nbsp;</p>
      <h3>Coins</h3>
      <p>The United States issues several denominations, with the most common being: 1¢, 5¢, 10¢, 25¢, 50¢, and $1. The U.S. Mint is responsible for manufacturing and circulating coins to pay for goods and services. It also issues <a href="http://catalog.usmint.gov/shop/gift-ideas/">collectible</a> and <a href="http://catalog.usmint.gov/coins/commemoratives/">commemorative coins</a> that honor a person, place, or event and are available for <a href="http://catalog.usmint.gov/">purchase</a>.</p>
      <h3>Mutilated Money</h3>
      <p>If you have paper money that is extremely&nbsp;damaged, you can <a href="http://www.bep.gov/services/currencyredemption.html">redeem it</a> with the Bureau of Engraving and Printing. Examples of damaged paper money include bills that are less than one half of the bill, or in such a condition that you're unable to tell the denomination of the bill. If you have paper money that is dirty, defaced, torn or more than half of the original size, you can take it to your local bank to exchange it for a bill that is in better shape.</p>
      <p>You can <a href="https://www.usmint.gov/news/consumer-alerts/mutilated-coin-program">redeem bent or partial coins</a> through the Mutilated Coin Redemption Program.</p>

      <header> 
       <h2 id="item-36527">Currency Exchange</h2> 
      </header> 
      <p>Each country has its own currency or monetary system for buying and selling products and services. The exchange rate between two countries tells you the price you pay to buy another country's currency. When you travel internationally, you will want to have some of that country's currency to buy products or services.&nbsp;</p>
      <h3>Exchanging Currency</h3>
      <p>Research the costs and process of purchasing currency for a&nbsp;foreign country:</p>
      <ul>
       <li>Use online&nbsp;currency conversion tools&nbsp;to compare the value of your country's currency with the value of other countries' currencies. Each week, the&nbsp;<a href="http://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/h10/current/">Federal Reserve Board</a>&nbsp;lists&nbsp; the&nbsp;currency value of over 20 countries against the value of the U.S. dollar. The rates are not in real time.&nbsp;</li>
       <li>Check with local banks to learn how to buy currency for another country.</li>
       <li>Use currency exchange booths, exchange vending machines, and ATMs at international airports or train stations. These booths may charge a fee for the exchange service.</li>
      </ul>
      <p>If you plan to use a credit card or ATM card abroad, the exchange rate may be different than the rates at currency exchange booths.</p>
      <h3>Cash Limits When Traveling Abroad or Entering the U.S.</h3>
      <ul>
       <li>Look up the&nbsp;<a href="https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/international-travel/International-Travel-Country-Information-Pages.html">country or countries you plan to visit</a>&nbsp;for their currency guidelines and country information.</li>
       <li>Find out if you must <a href="https://help.cbp.gov/app/answers/detail/a_id/195/~/currency-%2F-monetary-instruments---amount-that-can-be-brought-into-or-leave-the">declare the amount of money</a> you take out of the United States.</li>
      </ul>

           <p>Brian Thompson, Banknote Designer:<br>My job is to design&nbsp;<a href="https://www.uscurrency.gov/denominations" style="color: rgb(89, 89, 89);">United States currency.</a></p>
           <p>One thing about being a bank note designer is it's 98% thinking. You have to think about what you're going to do and think about what's going to work when it gets on the press. The easiest approach when designing, it's a big puzzle. You take different pieces and aspects of America or different things and piece it together almost like one united, almost like a story.</p>

           <p>There's not too many artists that could say that they've had their work replicated billions of times.</p>

First, the background images are printed. Then presses print the backs of the notes and then the faces of the notes. The final step is the printing of the serial number and&nbsp;<a href="https://www.treasury.gov/about/role-of-treasury/Pages/default.aspx" style="color: rgb(89, 89, 89);">Treasury</a>&nbsp;and Federal Reserve seals.</p>
