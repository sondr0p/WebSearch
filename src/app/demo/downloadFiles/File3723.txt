                </div> 
                <div class="blogText comment more">

                 <hr> 
                 <p><em>Dear Molly,</em></p> 
                 <p><em>Is there really a Starbucks at CIA Headquarters?</em></p> 
                 <p><em>~ Frap Fanatic</em></p> 
                 <hr> 
                 <p></p> 
                 <p>Dear Frap Fanatic,</p> 
                 <p>Yep, there is a Starbucks at CIA Headquarters! It’s actually one of the busiest Starbucks in the country. We need our caffeine jolt just like everyone else.</p> 
                 <p>The menu is the same as the Starbucks on your local street corner. They even play the same modern music, and we have a comfortable seating area for officers to take a break, hang with a co-worker, or talk shop… as long as the conversations are unclassified of course.</p> 
                 <p>The only big difference between this Starbucks and your regular Starbucks: Baristas don’t take customers’ names. Our officers order their cup of joe incognito.</p> 
                 <p>~ Molly</p>
                </div> 
               </div> 
               <span class="documentByLine"> </span>

                <div class="blogText comment more">
                 <h3>The Indomitable Woman</h3> 
                 <p><i>“All I knew, I wanted to help those guys in any way I could. I believe in freedom.”</i></p> 
                 <p><i>~ Maria Gulovich</i></p> 
                 <hr> 
                 <p></p>
                 <dl class="image-right captioned" style="width:200px;"> 
                  <dt>
                   <a rel="lightbox" href="/news-information/blog/2019/images-1/Gulovich-West-Point-1946.JPG/image.jpg"><img src="/news-information/blog/2019/images-1/Gulovich-West-Point-1946.JPG/c640be8d-6099-4c35-ac6a-8db91ef5e5d4.jpeg" alt="Gulovich-West-Point-1946.JPG" title="Gulovich-West-Point-1946.JPG" height="134" width="200"></a>
                  </dt> 
                  <dd class="image-caption" style="width:200px;">
                   Maria, the first woman to be honored with a review of cadets at West Point, receiving the Bronze Star, circa 1946.
                  </dd> 
                 </dl>Maria Gulovich, a young Slovakian schoolteacher, was only 23-years-old when she began harboring Jews from the Nazis. She joined the underground resistance and began working for the Office of Strategic Services (OSS) as a guide and interpreter. 
                 <p>Maria repeatedly risked her life to assist the OSS, including guiding a small group of American and British intelligence officers for nine weeks through the rugged mountains of Slovakia, in a blizzard, while being hunted by Nazis.</p> 
                 <p>Her bravery and her indomitable spirit caught the attention of OSS Director William "Wild Bill" Donovan and future CIA Director Allen Dulles, who helped Maria become a US citizen in 1952. Maria was also the first woman to be honored with a review of cadets at the historic US Military Academy at West Point, where she was awarded the Bronze Star for her heroic service on behalf of the United States.</p>

                 <p><em>Does CIA really have a library? If so, I would like a library card!</em></p> 
                 <p><em>~ Bookworm</em></p> 
                 <hr> 
                 <p></p> 
                 <p>Dear Bookworm,</p> 
                 <p>We do have a library! But to get a library card, you’ll need a security clearance. That’s because although CIA Library might look like any other modern public library, resting alongside the periodicals and stacks of books on history, international affairs, and science you’ll find volumes most Americans will never see. The literature of secrets.</p> 
                 <p>But our collection is mainly unclassified, and we have approximately 100,000 print materials and access to over 200 online databases that include more than 90,000 full-text electronic periodicals, dissertations, photographs, and public records.</p> 
                 <p>Whew, that’s a lot of information!</p> 
                 <p>We also have materials like maps, language resources, and movies. (Yep, we even have a DVD collection that includes spy movies and documentaries).</p> 
                 <p>We also have a few more “interesting” collections you won’t find at your neighborhood library.</p> 
                 <p>The Whaley Denial and Deception Collection contains materials on magic, lying, and even methods used by catchers and pitchers to communicate during baseball games.</p> 
                 <p>The <strong><a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/historical-intelligence-collection.html">Historical Intelligence Collection</a></strong> is basically a treasure trove of anything to do with the intelligence profession. We have more than 25,000 books and press clippings in the collection. The oldest item is a book on cryptography bound in vellum and published primarily in Latin in 1606!</p> 
                 <p>With all that information, it should come as no surprise then that we also have full-time<strong> <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2015-featured-story-archive/top-10-research-tips-for-a-great-school-year-from-cia-librarians.html">librarians</a> </strong>who work at CIA. They’re in high demand, and it’s not unusual for them to be recruited by other Agency offices because of their exceptional research abilities and training in information science.</p> 
                 <p>When most people think of CIA and the officers who help keep our country safe, a librarian is probably not what comes to mind. We think some heroes wear cardigans.</p> 
                 <p>~ Molly</p>

                 <span class="blogDate">Apr 01, 2019</span> 
                 <span class="blogDescription"> <span class="state-published url" href="/news-information/blog/2019/the-art-of-simple-sabotage">The Art of Simple Sabotage</span> </span> 
                </div> 
                <div class="blogText comment more">
                 <p>The rascally spies of OSS knew a thing or two about mischief making, especially when it came to undermining America’s enemies in World War II. One of their more imaginative ideas was to train everyday citizens in the art of simple sabotage.</p> 
                 <p>Thus, the “Simple Sabotage Field Manual” was born: <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2012-featured-story-archive/CleanedUOSSSimpleSabotage_sm.pdf">[<b>PDF 2.5 MB</b>]</a>.</p> 
                 <p>This previously classified booklet describes ways to train normal people to be purposely annoying telephone operators, dysfunctional train conductors, befuddling middle managers, blundering factory workers, unruly movie theater patrons, and so on. &nbsp;</p> 
                 <p>In other words, teaching people to do their jobs badly.</p> 
                 <p>OSS Director William “Wild Bill” Donovan had select parts of the manual declassified and disseminated to citizens of enemy states through pamphlets, targeted radio broadcasts, and in person.</p> 
                 <p>While the guidebook does contain ideas for serious, hardcore sabotage you’d expect during wartime, there are many timeless (and dare we say all-too-familiar) tactics that could drive even the most sane person batty.</p> 
                 <p><b>Here’s a list of five particularly timeless tips from the “Simple Sabotage Field Manual”: </b></p> 
                 <ol> 
                  <li><i>Telephone</i>: At the office, hotel, or local telephone switchboards, delay putting calls through, give out wrong numbers, cut people off “accidentally,” or forget to disconnect them so that the line cannot be used again.</li> 
                  <li><i>Movie Theater Patrons</i>: To ruin everyone’s time at the movies (without a cell phone, that is) bring in a paper bag filled with two or three dozen large moths. Open the bag and set it in an empty section of the theater. “The moths will fly out and climb into the projector beam, so that the film will be obscured by fluttering shadows.”</li> 
                  <li><i>Managers and Supervisors</i>: To lower morale and production, &nbsp;think of the worst boss you’ve had and act like that. Be pleasant to inefficient workers; give them undeserved promotions. Discriminate against efficient workers; complain unjustly about their work. When possible, refer all matters to committees for "further study and consideration." Attempt to make the committees as large and bureaucratic as possible.</li> 
                  <li><i>Employees</i>: Be forgetful. Clumsy. Work slowly. Think of ways to increase the number of movements needed to do your job: use a light hammer instead of a heavy one; try to make a small wrench do instead of a big one.</li> 
                  <li><i>Transportation</i>: Make train or air travel as inconvenient as possible. One particularly effective trick: issue two tickets for the same seat on a train in order to set up an “interesting” argument.</li> 
                 </ol>

                <div class="contenttype-agencypage blogSummary"> 
                 <span class="blogDate">Mar 22, 2019</span> 
                 <span class="blogDescription"> <span class="state-published url" href="/news-information/blog/2019/ask-molly-march-22-2019">Ask Molly: March 22, 2019 </span> </span> 
                </div> 
                <div class="blogText comment more">
                 <p><img src="/news-information/blog/2019/Molly_banner.jpg/image.jpg" class="image-inline" alt="" title=""></p> 
                 <hr> 
                 <p><em>Dear Molly,</em></p> 
                 <p><em>How many CIA women have died in the line of duty?</em></p> 
                 <p><em>~ Fallen Stars</em></p> 
                 <hr> 
                 <p></p> 
                 <p>Dear Fallen Stars,</p> 
                 <p>We have an ivory-white marble wall in our lobby at CIA Headquarters that stands as a silent, simple memorial to honor the women and men who have given their lives in service to our country. Currently, there are 129 stars carved into the marble of the <a class="internal-link" href="/library/publications/resources/cia-memorial-wall-publication/index.html"><strong>CIA Memorial Wall: </strong></a>91 are unclassified. Of those, 11 represent women.</p> 
                 <p>Officially, the first female CIA officer to die in the line of duty and receive a star on the Memorial Wall was <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2013-featured-story-archive/remembering-barbara-robbins.html"><b>Barbara Robbins</b></a>. She was killed only two years after joining the Agency – in March 1965 – when terrorists bombed the US Embassy in South Vietnam. She remains the youngest CIA officer to receive a star, at just 21-years old.</p> 
                 <p>However, what many Agency history buffs don’t know is that the first ever CIA officer to die while working for the Agency was also a woman: her name was <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2016-featured-story-archive/mystery-of-jane-wallis-burrell.html"><b>Jane Wallis Burrell</b></a>.</p> 
                 <p>At a time when most women in US intelligence worked in clerical roles, Jane was a CIA counterintelligence officer who served in all of CIA’s predecessor agencies: the <a class="internal-link" href="/library/publications/intelligence-history/oss/index.htm"><span class="internal-link"><b>Office of Strategic Services</b></span></a>, the <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2010-featured-story-archive/moving-up-to-the-big-leagues.html"><b>Strategic Services Unit</b></a>, and the <a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/2015-featured-story-archive/cloak-and-dagger-the-unexpected-beginnings-of-cia.html"><b>Central Intelligence Group</b></a>.</p> 
                 <p>On January 6, 1948, an Air France flight from Brussels crashed on its way to Paris, killing all five crew members and 10 of the 11 passengers. Among the dead was a young woman who the press said was either a clerk or a courier. She was neither. Jane was a CIA officer, and her death—only 110 days after CIA was officially established the previous September—makes her the first CIA officer to die while employed by the Agency.</p> 
                 <p>We know don’t know much about Jane’s activity at the time of her death. She was returning from a trip to Brussels, but there are no records to indicate whether or not she was on vacation or an official operation.</p> 
                 <p>Jane was not a candidate for a star on the Memorial Wall because the wall commemorates Agency employees who died in specific circumstances: deaths from accidental crashes of commercial aircraft have generally not qualified. Still, her service with CIA and its predecessor organizations was honorable and she deserves to be remembered.</p> 
                 <p>When any CIA officer (male or female) dies in the line of duty, their names frequently must be kept secret. Sometimes, the first time that families hear that their loved one worked for the Agency is when that officer has died. However, with the passage of time, we’ve been able to unveil many of the fallen, and share their heroic stories with the public. If you’re interested in reading more about the lives of our fallen officers, see our “<a class="internal-link" href="/news-information/featured-story-archive/index"><b>Feature Story</b></a>” section on CIA.gov and look for articles from our “Remembering CIA's Heroes” series.</p> 
                 <p>~ Molly</p>

                 <p>People from nearly every country share information with CIA, and new individuals contact us daily. If you have information you think might interest CIA due to our foreign intelligence collection mission, there are many ways to reach us. </p> 
                 <p>If you know of an <b>imminent threat</b> to a location inside the U.S., immediately contact your local law enforcement or <a href="https://www.fbi.gov/contact-us/field-offices"><b>FBI Field Office</b></a>. For threats outside the U.S., <a href="#" class="contactTriggerFromReport"><b>contact CIA</b></a> or go to a U.S. Embassy or Consulate and ask for the information to be passed to a U.S. official. Please know, CIA does not engage in law enforcement.</p> 
                 <p>In addition to the options below, individuals contact CIA in a variety of creative ways. The best method depends on your personal situation. We will work to protect all information you provide, including your identity, and our interactions with you will be respectful and professional. Depending on what you provide, we may offer you compensation.</p> 
                 <h3>WHAT TO PROVIDE</h3> 
                 <p>If you feel it is safe, consider providing these details with your submission:</p> 
                 <span style="display:block"> 
                  <ul style="padding:0 0 0 40px;width:100;%;list-style:disc outside none"> 
                   <li> Your full name</li> 
                   <li> Biographic details, such as a photograph of yourself, and a copy of the biographic page of your passport </li> 
                   <li> How you got the information you want to share with CIA</li> 
                   <li> How to contact you, including your home address and phone number</li> 
                  </ul> </span> 
                 <p>We cannot guarantee a response to every message. We reply first to messages of greater interest to us and to those with more detail. Our response will occur via a secure method.</p> 
                 <h3>WAYS TO SUBMIT</h3> 
                 <p><b>Internet:</b> Send a message <a class="contactTriggerFromReport" href="#"><b>here</b></a>. Or, you can send us a message using the <a class="external-link" href="https://www.torproject.org/">Tor browser</a> at <strong>ciadotgov4sjwlzihbbgxnqg3xiyrg7so2r2o3lt5wz5ypk4sxyjstad.onion</strong>. We go to great lengths to keep these channels secure, but any communication via the internet poses some risk. Using the Tor browser, a virtual private network, and/or a device not registered to you can reduce some risk.</p> 
                 <p><b>Mail:</b> Inside the U.S., send mail to the following address:</p> 
                 <blockquote>
                  <p>Central Intelligence Agency<br> Office of Public Affairs <br> Washington, D.C. 20505</p>
                 </blockquote> 
                 <p>You can also mail a letter to a U.S. Embassy or Consulate and request it be forwarded to CIA. Please note we have no control over the security and reliability of postal mail.</p> 
                 <p><b>In-Person:</b> Outside the U.S., go to a U.S. Embassy or Consulate and inform a U.S. official you have information for CIA.</p> 
                 <p><b>Third Party:</b> Have someone you trust travel to a less restrictive environment and deliver the information via one of the above methods.</p>
