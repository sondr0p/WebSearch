           <h1>National Archives Frequently Asked Questions</h1> 
           <p>These are introductory answers to frequently asked questions about the National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) and our holdings and services.</p> 
           <ul> 
            <li>Select a subject from the menu on the left to view questions and answers relating to your selection.</li> 
            <li>Links will guide you to further information on our website or to other sources.</li> 
           </ul> 
           <h2><a id="about" name="about"></a>About the National Archives</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><span><strong>What is the National Archives ? </strong></span></p> 
            <p>The National Archives and Records Administration (NARA) is the nation's record keeper. Of all documents and materials created in the course of business conducted by the United States Federal government, only 1%-3% are so important for legal or historical reasons that they are kept by us forever.</p> 
            <p>Those valuable records are preserved and are available to you, whether you want to see if they contain clues about your family's history, need to prove a veteran's military service, or are researching a historical topic that interests you.</p> 
            <p><a href="#visiting">Visiting the National Archives</a></p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <h2>Doing Research</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I do research at the Archives? </span></strong></p> 
            <p><a href="/research/start/faqs.html">Learn more about how to do research at the National Archives</a>.&nbsp;</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <h2><a name="borrowing"></a>Borrowing NARA Materials</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>Does NARA lend original documents for research? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA does not lend original documents for research use.<br> <br> Originating Federal agencies or successors in function; courts; and the President, Vice President, former Presidents or Vice Presidents or donors, or their designated representative may request loans of their own original holdings for the conduct of official business. Loans to originating agencies are limited to instances of demonstrated need when copies will not suffice and are subject to conditions that exempt from loan any holdings of high intrinsic value or in need of preservation action.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Does NARA lend original documents for exhibition use?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Yes, original documents or artifacts may be loaned to qualified institutions for exhibition when the purpose of the loan is to inform and educate the public about NARA, its holdings, or the national experience while ensuring their continued availability for the future. Exhibitions must be accessible to the public and may not be primarily political or commercial.<br> <br> Borrowers should submit a written request 180 days prior to the loan date and include an <a href="http://www.aam-us.org/">American Alliance of Museums</a> or equivalent. NARA's security, fire protection, environmental, and transport requirements are intended to preserve and protect NARA's holdings and borrowers are expected to comply with them.</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <ul> 
            <li><a href="/exhibits/borrowing/">Read the complete guidelines for borrowing materials</a></li> 
           </ul> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="german-records"></a>Captured German Records and the Berlin Document Center</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>What is the Berlin Document Center?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>The records of the Berlin Document Center consist of personnel and related records of the Nazi party (NSDAP) and its affiliated organizations and activities from the founding of the Party in 1920 until 1945.<br> <br> NARA holds more than 70,000 rolls of microfilm reproducing <a href="/research/captured-german-records/">captured German and related records</a>. Reference copies of the microfilm may be viewed free of charge in the Microfilm Research Room, <a href="/dc-metro/college-park/index.html">National Archives at College Park</a>, 8601 Adelphi Road, College Park, MD 20740-6001.<br> Self-service copies from microfilm can also be made in the research room.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I get the records for someone who was in the SS or a member of the Nazi party?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>This information can be located in the <a href="/research/captured-german-records/">captured German and related records</a>.</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="census"></a>Census Records</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I get census records?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Federal population census records, 1790-1930, are available for research at the <a href="/dc-metro/washington/index.html">National Archives Building in Washington, DC</a>, and in <a href="/locations/">Regional&nbsp;Facilities</a>&nbsp;throughout the country. The National Archives at College Park does not have census records.</p> 
            <p>Please note that the <a href="/research/census/1940/">1940 census</a> is the most recent one available for research. You can also access information about the <a href="/research/census/1930/">1930 census</a>.</p> 
            <p>Some libraries and other research institutions have purchased copies of Federal censuses. Check with your local library or genealogical society to see if the census may be available in your area.</p> 
            <strong><span>Can I order copies of census materials by mail?</span></strong> 
            <p>NARA will only copy exactly identified pages of the Federal census. To use this service, you must provide the following:</p> 
            <ul> 
             <li>census year</li> 
             <li>name of the individual listed</li> 
             <li>exact page number</li> 
             <li>state</li> 
             <li>county</li> 
             <li>enumeration district (1880-1920 only)</li> 
            </ul> 
            <p>Copies of the exact census page can now be <a href="https://eservices.archives.gov/orderonline/start.swe?SWECmd=GotoView&amp;SWEView=GPEA+Topic+Sub-topic+View+FFO&amp;SWEPostnApplet=GPEA+Topic+Information+List+Applet+FFO&amp;SWEPostnRowId=1-C27NI">ordered online</a>, as well as through the <a href="/contact/inquire-form.html">NATF Form 82</a> (National Archives Order for Copies of Census Records).</p> 
            <p><strong><span>What if I don't know the exact page of the census?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>You might be able to find census indexes near you. Check with your local librarian or genealogical society.             <p>From these indexes, you can determine the exact page on which a family was enumerated. With that information, you can use the NATF Form 82 to order a copy of the page. Use the online <a href="/contact/inquire-form.html">Inquire form</a> to request NATF Form 82.</p>

           <h2><a name="copyright"></a>Copyright</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>May I reproduce images from your website?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>The vast majority of the digital images in the <a href="https://catalog.archives.gov/">National Archives Catalog</a> are in the public domain.             <p><strong><span>Can I get a signed permission form from NARA to use materials?</span></strong></p>

Please <a href="/contact/inquire-form.html">contact us</a> if you have questions.</p>

            <blockquote>
             "Only the bound volumes of the United States Reports contain the final, official text of the opinions of the Supreme Court of the United States. In case of discrepancies between the bound volume and any other version of a case--whether print or electronic, official or unofficial--the bound volume controls."

           <h2><a name="digitizing-projects"></a>Digitizing Projects at the National Archives</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>Can you tell me about digitizing projects going on at the National Archives?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA recognizes that the expectation of easy online access to our holdings continues to grow. Research is no longer relegated to libraries and research rooms but is being done around-the-clock on computers around the world. To meet this need, we will create, to the greatest extent possible, an “archives without walls.”</p> 
            <p>We plan to create digital versions of selected records, including those most requested by researchers. Digitizing materials from our holdings will improve access to those holdings and will help preserve and protect the original materials from excessive handling.</p> 
            <p>To help achieve those goals, we are in discussions with several private companies and non-profit organizations to explore mutually-beneficial opportunities to digitize -- and make available -- our holdings. The resulting non-exclusive partnerships will become an important component of our effort to further expand online public access to our nation’s archival records.</p> 
            <p>As we expand and enter into more of these partnerships, we will provide news about these pilot and longer-term projects; see more information about <a href="/digitization/">Digitization at the National Archives</a>.</p>

           <h2><a name="genealogy"></a>Genealogy</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>How can NARA help me with genealogy research?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>The National Archives is a treasure trove of materials that can be used to trace your family lineage. More information is available on <a href="/research/genealogy/start-research/">Beginning Your Genealogical Research</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I get started with genealogy?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Experts often suggest beginning with your oldest living relatives. Find out more on <a href="/research/genealogy/start-research/">Beginning Your Genealogical Research</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I find ancestors of Native American descent?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA holds a great deal of information useful in tracing <a href="/research/native-americans/research-individual-more.html">Native American descent.</a></p> 
            <p><strong><span>Where can I get a copy of my ancestor's passport</span>?</strong><br> <br> Passport applications can be an excellent source of genealogical information, especially about foreign-born individuals. NARA has passport applications from October 1795 through March 1925; the U.S. Department of State has passport applications from April 1925 to the present. More on <a href="/research/passport/index.html">Passport Applications</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>What is Soundex, and how does it work?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>The Soundex is a coded surname (last name) index based on the way a surname sounds rather than the way it is spelled. Surnames that sound the same, but are spelled differently, like SMITH and SMYTH, have the same code and are filed together. The Soundex coding system was developed so that you can find a surname even though it may have been recorded under various spellings. <a href="/research/census/soundex.html">More information on Soundex.</a></p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I research my family name?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA is not able to help you with broad research on a family name. However, you can <a href="/research/hire-help/">hire an independent researcher</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I research when my family entered the country?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>You can find out about <a href="/research/immigration/passenger-arrival.html">NARA's immigration records holdings</a> in the genealogy section's <a href="/research/immigration/index.html">Immigration Records</a> or <a href="/research/naturalization/naturalization.html">Naturalization Records</a>. When researching in this area, working backward from the present should provide you with a manageable time frame in which to conduct research.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Where can I get copies of ship passenger lists?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA has some <a href="/research/immigration/passenger-arrival.html#have">ship passenger arrival records</a>.<br> <br> There are many other sources for these records, including an <a href="http://www.loc.gov/rr/genealogy/bib_guid/immigrant/">online guide at the Library of Congress</a>. Your local library, historical society, or genealogical society can be helpful. For online research, simply begin by choosing a search engine and entering the words "ship passenger lists."</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Can I see Ellis Island or other immigration records online?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA does not hold those records, but some Ellis Island records are available online from the <a href="http://www.ellisislandrecords.org/">Ellis Island Foundation</a>.</p> 
            <p><span><strong>How do I get a copy of a Homestead application?</strong></span></p>

            <p>Reproductions of land entry files (such as credit, cash, homestead, and mineral) or surrendered military bounty-land warrants files (Acts of 1788, 1812, 1847, 1850, and 1855) can now be <a href="https://eservices.archives.gov/orderonline/start.swe?SWECmd=GotoView&amp;SWEScreen=GPEA+Microfilm+MIF&amp;SWEView=GPEA+Microfilm+Landing+Page+View+MIF">ordered online</a>, as well as through the <a href="/contact/inquire-form.html#part-a">NATF Form 84</a>.</p>

            <p>The <a href="/publications/prologue/2000/fall/us-canada-immigration-records-1.html">Fall 2000 issue of NARA's <em>Prologue </em>magazine</a> describes the materials available for this topic. Please see the information on NARA's holdings related to Canadian border crossings.</p>

            <p>Please see the information on NARA's holdings related to <a href="/research/immigration/border-mexico.html">Mexican border crossings</a>.</p>

           <h2><a name="presidential"></a>Presidential Materials</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>I'm interested in Presidential materials such as speeches, proclamations, Executive Orders, etc. Where can I find them?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Presidential materials are first published in the daily <a href="/federal-register/publications/index.html"><em>Federal Register</em></a>. They are then issued by the Office of the Federal Register in the <em> <a href="/federal-register/publications/presidential-compilation.html">Weekly Compilation of Presidential Documents</a></em>. The <em>Weekly Compilation</em> is published every Monday by the Office of the Federal Register, and contains statements, messages, and other Presidential materials released by the White House during the preceding week. The <a href="http://www.whitehouse.gov">White House website</a> is also a useful source for current Presidential documents.</p> 
            <p>Presidential materials are codified as Title 3 of the <em> <a href="/federal-register/cfr/">Code of Federal Regulations</a>.</em> The publication is available in both electronic and print formats and can be found in <a href="http://www.gpo.gov/libraries/">Federal Depository Libraries</a>. The online <em>Weekly Compilation</em> is full-text beginning with the Clinton Administration.</p> 
            <p>Executive Orders (EOs) can be difficult to research. The full text of EOs is available online beginning with the Clinton Administration. Bear in mind, however, that they are not static documents. They often change over time. In addition, they can be repealed or superseded by subsequent Executive Orders. The <a href="/federal-register/executive-orders/disposition.html">Executive Orders disposition tables</a> on this site can be very helpful in locating an Executive Order and in determining its current status.</p> 
            <p>Executive Orders published between April 13, 1945, and January 20, 1989 can be found in the publication <em> <a href="/federal-register/codification/index.html">Codification of Presidential Proclamations and Executive Orders</a></em>. This document is out of print, but in addition to the online version, it can be found in most <a href="http://www.gpo.gov/libraries/">Federal Depository Libraries.</a></p> 
            <p><a href="http://www.gpo.gov/libraries/">Find a Federal Depository Library</a> near you.</p> 
            <p>Executive Orders and Presidential Proclamations have also been commercially indexed and filmed on microfiche by the Congressional Information Service (CIS index to presidential Executive Orders &amp; proclamations. Washington, DC: Congressional Information Service, 1986-). These indexes and/or microfiche may be available in a local library.</p> 
            <p>Or <a href="/presidential-libraries/">visit a Presidential Library</a> and learn more about their holdings and educational programs.</p> 
           </blockquote>

             <div class="dynamic-widget">
              <p><a class="moreLink" href="https://www.nationalarchivesstore.org/?utm_campaign=2019eStore&amp;utm_medium=website&amp;utm_source=nara&amp;utm_content=organic">Shop the <strong>National Archives Store</strong></a></p> 
             </div> 
            </div> 
            <p><strong><span>Where can I get copies (reproductions) of items displayed in one of your exhibits (online or not)? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>NARA sells reproductions of some of the materials it exhibits. The <a href="/publications/">Publications section</a> describes the items available and provides ordering instructions.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Do you sell exhibit catalogs? </span></strong></p> Yes. NARA does sell 
            <a href="/publications/exhibit-catalogs.html">catalogs</a> for many of its exhibits.
           </blockquote> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="treaties"></a>Treaties</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>Where can I find the text of a treaty?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>The printed series <em>U.S . Treaties and Other International Agreements</em> is the best source. It is not online, but should be available in most <a href="http://www.gpo.gov/libraries/">Federal Depository Libraries</a>.</p> 
            <p>Until 1948, treaties passed by the U.S. Senate appeared in the Statutes at Large, which should also be available in a depository library.</p> 
            <p>Various other compilations and sources also exist. These may be available in a local library. Other single treaties may be available online and can be found using your favorite search engine.</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="us-code"></a>United States Code</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong>What is the U.S. Code?</strong></p> 
            <p>The U.S. Code is a consolidation and codification by subject matter of the general and permanent laws of the United States.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How do I get to an online copy of the U.S. Code? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>A searchable version can be found on the <a href="https://uscode.house.gov/">Office of the Law Revision Counsel</a> website at the U.S. House of Representatives. The Code is also available for sale by the <a href="https://www.gpo.gov/fdsys/browse/collectionUScode.action?collectionCode=USCODE">U.S. Government Publishing Office</a> in a variety of formats.</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="website"></a>Using the Website</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>How can I find something specific on your website? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>We have tried to organize the website to make our most often accessed offerings easily findable via browsing.&nbsp;<br> <br> In addition, we have made many improvements to our search engine to make it an effective and efficient means of locating information. <a href="/global-pages/subject-index.html">Search the site</a>.<br> <br> If you have any problems with our website, please <a href="/contact/inquire-form.html#part-c">contact us</a> so that we can make any necessary improvements.<br> &nbsp;</p>

           </blockquote>

           <h2><a name="visiting"></a>Visiting and Using the National Archives</h2> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <p><strong><span></span></strong></p> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>Where is the National Archives located?</span> </strong></p> 
            <p>The headquarters of the National Archives is located in Washington DC. In addition, a system of Regional Records Services facilities and Presidential libraries spans the entire country. Information on locations and hours can be found at <a href="/locations/index.html">NARA Facilities</a>.</p> 
            <p><a href="/dc-metro/washington/">National Archives Building in Washington, DC</a></p> 
            <p><a href="/dc-metro/college-park/">National Archives at College Park, MD </a></p> 
            <p><strong><span>How can I best use the National Archives for research? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>Often research can be conducted in local libraries or historical societies. Our <a href="/research/start/index.html">Getting Started</a> document can explain differences and similarities between NARA and libraries.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>How can I best prepare to conduct research in the National Archives? </span></strong></p> 
            <p>To best use your time at NARA, please review our <a href="/research/start/plan-visit.html">tips for planning your visit</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Who can use the National Archives?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Anyone can use the National Archives. You do not need to be an American citizen or to present credentials or a letter of recommendation. Please refer to the synopsis of the <a href="/research/start/nara-regulations.html">Regulations for Using the National Archives</a> or to the complete <a href="/about/regulations/">NARA regulations as published in the Code of Federal Regulations</a>.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Can I bring and use cameras, scanners, and laptops?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>In the Washington area, you may bring equipment. All bags and carrying cases must be left in lockers outside of the research room.<br> <br> Cameras may be used only with natural light.<br> <br> Flatbed scanners without sheet feeders are allowed. When you are using a scanner, you must show it to the research room staff and receive special instructions. <a href="/research/order/scanning.html">More information on using scanners</a>.</p> 
            <p>Contact <a href="/locations/">each facility directly</a> for their policies on equipment.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>Can I use my pen and notebook?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Because of the fragility and irreplaceability of many archival materials, restrictions for their protection are in place. We will provide blank paper and pencils.</p> 
            <p><strong><span>When can I do research?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>Contact <a href="/locations/">Regional Records Services Facilities</a> and <a href="/presidential-libraries/index.html">Presidential Libraries</a> directly for their hours of operation.</p> 
           </blockquote> 
           <p>&nbsp;</p> 
           <hr> 
           <h2><a name="vital-records"></a>Vital Records</h2> 
           <blockquote> 
            <p><strong><span>What are vital records?</span></strong></p> 
            <p>"Vital records" most commonly refers to records such as birth and death certificates, marriage licenses and divorce decrees, wills and the like. These records are created by local authorities, and with possible exceptions for events overseas, in the military, or in the District of Columbia. They are not considered Federal records; therefore they are not held by NARA. For more information:</p> 
            <ul> 
             <li><a href="/research/alic/reference/vital-records.html">Library resources for vital records</a><br> &nbsp;</li> 
             <li>Centers for Disease Control <a href="http://www.cdc.gov/nchs/howto/w2w/w2welcom.htm">resources for vital records</a></li> 
            </ul> 
            <p>In a Federal records management context, the term "vital records" refers to records essential to an agency's continued operations during a national emergency. NARA provides an instructional guide, <a href="/records-mgmt/vital-records/">Vital Records and Records Disaster Mitigation and Recovery</a>, to assist agencies with developing and implementing a vital records program...</p> 
           </blockquote>
