          <h1 class="documentFirstHeading">Freedom of Information Act Electronic Reading Room</h1> 
          <div class="region region-content"> 
           <div id="block-boxes-homepage-combined-content" class="block block-boxes block-boxes-simple clearfix"> 
            <div class="content"> 
             <div id="boxes-box-homepage_combined_content" class="boxes-box">
              <div class="boxes-box-content">
               <p>Welcome to the Central Intelligence Agency's Freedom of Information Act Electronic Reading Room.<br><br> Do UFOs fascinate you? Are you a history buff who wants to learn more about the Bay of Pigs, Vietnam or the A-12 Oxcart? Have stories about spies always fascinated you? You can find information about all of these topics and more in the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) Electronic Reading Room.</p> 
               <h1><a href="what-electronic-reading-room">What is the Electronic Reading Room?</a></h1> 
               <blockquote>
                <p class="documentFirstHeading"><a style="line-height: 1.53em; font-size: 1.15em; font-weight: bold;" href="foia-annual-report">CIA Annual FOIA Reports</a></p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <blockquote>
                <p>(Updated April 22, 2019)<br><br> The CIA FOIA Annual Report is now available in&nbsp;<a href="/library/readingroom/docs/CIA_FY2018_FOIA_Annual_Report.pdf">PDF</a>, and in&nbsp;<a href="/library/readingroom/docs/CIA_FY2018_FOIA_Annual_Report.xml">machine-readable XML</a>&nbsp;formats.</p>
               </blockquote> 
              </div>

              <div class="boxes-box-content">
               <h1>What's New on the Electronic Reading Room?</h1> 
               <blockquote>
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 1.25em; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/currentcentral-intelligence-bulletin">Current Intelligence Bulletin Collection </a></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/currentcentral-intelligence-bulletin"><img style="width: 473px; height: 420px; margin-right: 3px; margin-left: 1px;" alt="CIB" src="/library/readingroom/images/CIB_3.jpg"></a></p> 
                <p><strong><u>Current Intelligence Bulletin</u></strong></p> 
                <p>Harry Truman was the first U.S. president to receive a daily intelligence digest. At his direction, the Daily Summary began production in February 1946, and continued until February 1951. President Truman was pleased with the product, but a survey group commissioned by the National Security Council in 1949 was critical of the Daily Summary and issued several recommendations to improve it. The "new and improved" version, called the Current Intelligence Bulletin, began production on 28 February 1951. This remained the format of the president's daily digest through Dwight Eisenhower's two terms, although it was titled the Central Intelligence Bulletin from 1958-1961. The Current/Central Intelligence Bulletin grew longer than its predecessor over time with the addition of more items and more analysis, and would eventually contain more graphics as printing technology improved.</p> 
                <p><strong><u><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/currentcentral-intelligence-bulletin?page=12">1 January-30 June 1952</a></u></strong></p> 
                <p>On 6 February 1952, Princess Elizabeth acceded to the throne as Queen Elizabeth II. The major issue covered in the daily intellegence reports, however, remained the Korean War. Fighting continued along the Korean peninsula during this period in an effective stalemate, with little territory changing hands, and ceasefire negotiations, begun in 1951, still bore no fruit. Anticolonial sentiment continued to grow in French Indochina and began to become a significant factor in British Kenya.</p> 
                <p>This historical release includes: the Current Intelligence Bulletin reports from 1 January-30 June 1952 (1,082 pages)</p> 
                <p>This release is the <b>third</b> in a multi-part monthly series. Check back around this time next month to see more reports from the president's digest.</p> 
                <p>See <a href="/library/readingroom/collection/currentcentral-intelligence-bulletin">the Current/Central Intelligence Bulletin Collection</a></p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <hr style="border: currentColor; border-image: none; width: 94%; height: 1px; color: rgb(51, 51, 51); background-color: rgb(51, 51, 51);">
               <blockquote>
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 1.25em; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/tet-declassified">Tet Declassified</a></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/tet-declassified"><img style="width: 200px; height: 300px; margin-right: 3px; margin-left: 1px;" alt="Tet Declassified Vol. 3" src="/library/readingroom/images/Tet_Vol_3.jpg"></a></p> 
                <p>In concert with the DNI's <strong><i>third</i></strong> in a three-part installment on the Vietnam War's Tet Offensive and in recognition of its 50<sup style="top: -0.35em; line-height: 0; font-size: 0.85em; vertical-align: baseline; position: relative;">th</sup> anniversary, the CIA reviewed and contributed 235 documents associated with the Tet Offensive for the DNI's <strong><i>April 2019</i></strong> release.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <hr style="border: currentColor; border-image: none; width: 94%; height: 1px; color: rgb(51, 51, 51); background-color: rgb(51, 51, 51);">
               <blockquote>
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 1.25em; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/argentina-declassification-project-dirty-war-1976-83">Argentina Declassification Project - The "Dirty War" (1976-83)</a></p> 
                <p>During the Argentine government's seven year (1976-83) campaign against suspected dissidents and subversives, often know as the "Dirty War", between 10,000 and 30,000 people were killed, including opponents of the government as well as innocent victims. Responding to a White House directive, the CIA declassified and is now releasing documents relating to the "Dirty War" period in Argentina.</p> 
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 0.9em; font-weight: 500;">**Note: CIA focused its review on Argentina only information. Anything marked with "Page Denied" and /or "NR" has been deemed not relevant to the Argentina Project, whether or not it has been previously released. Other information can be consulted on the CIA's Electronic Reading Room or by submitting a FOIA request.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <hr style="border: currentColor; border-image: none; width: 94%; height: 1px; color: rgb(51, 51, 51); background-color: rgb(51, 51, 51);">
               <blockquote>
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 1.25em; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/daily-summary">Daily Summary Collection</a></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/daily-summary" target="_blank"><img style="width: 576px; height: 240px; margin-right: 3px; margin-left: 1px;" alt="Daily Summary" src="/library/readingroom/images/TheDailySummary.jpg"></a></p> 
                <p><strong><u>Do You Know What Came Before the PDB?</u></strong></p> 
                <p>You probably know that the CIA provides the President of the United States a summary of critical intelligence issues every day. But did you know that this was happening even before there was a CIA? The Central Intelligence Agency was not formally established until 1947. In January 1946, however, President Harry Truman directed the newly-formed Central Intelligence Group to provide him with a coordinated intelligence report known as the Daily Summary. This report evolved over the years and its name has changed its now called the <a href="/library/readingroom/presidents-daily-brief"><b><u> Presidents Daily Brief or PDB</u></b></a> but the tradition begun in 1946 of informing the President with a coordinated daily report continues to this day. Once for the Presidents eyes only (and those of his most senior advisors), these reports can now be released to the public.</p> 
                <p><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/daily-summary"><b><u>See Daily Summary reports from 1946-1951</u></b></a></p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <hr style="border: currentColor; border-image: none; width: 94%; height: 1px; color: rgb(51, 51, 51); background-color: rgb(51, 51, 51);">
               <blockquote>
                <p style="text-align: left; font-size: 1.15em; font-weight: bold;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/president-george-h-w-bushs-farewell-visit-cia">President George H. W. Bush's Farewell Visit to CIA</a></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><a href="/library/readingroom/collection/president-george-h-w-bushs-farewell-visit-cia"><img style="width: 204px; height: 280px; margin-right: 3px; margin-left: 1px;" alt="President George H. W. Bush" src="/library/readingroom/images/PresidentGHWBush.jpg"></a></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><strong>With gratitude and respect</strong></p> 
                <p style="text-align: left;"><strong>From the men and women of the Central Intelligence Agency</strong></p> 
               </blockquote>
