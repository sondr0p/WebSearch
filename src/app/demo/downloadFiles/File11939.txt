           <h1>Learn about Presidential Libraries</h1> 
           <!-- Content shared with ALIC --> 
           <p id="shared-alic">The Presidential Library system is composed of fourteen Presidential Libraries. These facilities are overseen by the <a href="/presidential-libraries/about/office.html">Office of Presidential Libraries</a>, in the National Archives and Records Administration.<br> </p> 
           <p>Presidential Libraries are archives and museums, bringing together the documents and artifacts of a President and his administration and presenting them to the public for study and discussion without regard for political considerations or affiliations. Presidential Libraries and Museums, like their holdings, belong to the American people.</p>

           <p>Many Presidential papers and records had been lost, destroyed, sold for profit, or ruined by poor storage conditions. In 1939, President Franklin D. Roosevelt sought a better alternative.&nbsp; <a href="/presidential-libraries/about/history.html">Learn more about the Presidential Libraries History</a></p> 
           <p> Congress legislated this policy, passing the Presidential Libraries Act in 1955. Through archives, museums, and public programs, Presidential Libraries continue to preserve the documents and artifacts of our Presidents, helping us learn about our nation and our democracy.&nbsp; <a href="/presidential-libraries/laws/">Learn more about Laws and Regulations</a></p> 
          </section>
