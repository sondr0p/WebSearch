            <h1>Intelligence Literature: Suggested Reading List</h1> 
            <div id="content-core"> 
             <div id="parent-fieldname-text-c89e21cc73cc54a50e888e8fac5d7e31" class=""> 
              <p><img src="/library/intelligence-literature/intell.jpg/image.jpg" class="image-inline" alt="Intelligence Literature" title="intell.jpg"></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p></p> 
              <p>This brief bibliography of intelligence literature provides a wide spectrum of views on intelligence and the Central Intelligence Agency. The readings cover history, technology, opinion, and some of the key personalities associated with intelligence. The book lists offer the reader personal and academic views on intelligence, its role in national security, and the forces that have shaped it over the years.</p> 
              <p>Last updated April 2011</p> 
              <ul> 
               <ul> 
                <li><a href="#world-war-ii-before" title="World War II &amp; Before">World War II &amp; Before</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#cia-oss-history" title="CIA &amp; OSS History">CIA &amp; OSS History</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#bio">Biographies &amp; Memoirs (CIA Careers)</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#women">Women in Intelligence</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#espionage">Espionage</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#counter">Operations: Counterintelligence (CI)</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#covert">Operations: Covert Action (CA)</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#analysis">Analysis</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#tech">Technology</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#war">War on Terrorism</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#general">General Interest</a></li> 
                <li><a href="#reference">Reference</a></li> 
               </ul> 
              </ul> 
              <p></p> 
              <p>This is not intended to be a complete list of works on intelligence, and it will be updated as needed.</p> 
              <p>Inclusion of a work on the list does not imply endorsement by the US Government or any of its agencies or branches.</p> 
              <p>Questions, suggestions and comments are welcomed and should be sent to: <a class="internal-link" title="Contact CIA" href="/contact-cia/index.html">Contact Us</a></p> 
              <p><b>Note: </b>Central Intelligence Agency publications are linked to those books on our website.</p>

                <p>New York: Alfred A. Knopf, 2004.</p> 
                <p>A contrasting look between the reality versus the fictional treatment of espionage from a former CIA officer's point of view.</p>

                <p>A collection of articles that discuss the major issues of strategic intelligence—covert action, espionage, liaison with foreign services, and the problems of politicalization.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <p><b>Scott Koch and Brian D. Fila</b></p> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p><a class="internal-link" title="index" href="/library/center-for-the-study-of-intelligence/csi-publications/books-and-monographs/our-first-line-of-defense-presidential-reflections-on-us-intelligence/index.html">Our First Line of Defense, Presidential Reflections.</a></p> 
                <p>Washington, D.C.: Center for the Study of Intelligence, 1996.</p> 
                <p>A compilation of presidential opinions on U.S. intelligence.</p> 
               </blockquote>

               <blockquote> 
                <p>A World of Secrets: The Uses and Limits of Intelligence.</p> 
                <p>New York: Basic Books, 1985.</p> 
                <p>This work takes a critical look at how intelligence is used to understand foreign events that may affect the U.S.</p> 
               </blockquote>

               <blockquote> 
                <p>Intelligence: From Secrets to Policy.</p> 
                <p>Washington: Congressional Quarterly Press, 2006. (3rd ed.)</p> 
                <p>An informative discussion about intelligence and the intelligence community.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <b>Count de Alexandre Marenches</b> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>The Fourth World War: Diplomacy and Espionage in the Age of Terrorism.</p> 
                <p>New York: William Morrow and Company, 1992.</p> 
                <p>Count de Alexandre de Marenches, the longest serving chief of French Intelligence gives a long-range forecast for the future of intelligence gathering.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <p><b>James Olson</b></p> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>Fair Play: The Moral Dilemmas of Spying.</p> 
                <p>Washington, DC: Potomac BOoks, 2006.</p> 
                <p>An illuminating treatment of real issues confronted by intelligence officers.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <b>Mark Riebling</b> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>Wedge: The Secret War Between the FBI and CIA.</p> 
                <p>New York: Alfred A. Knopf, 1994.</p> 
                <p>The title refers to the arbitrary division between domestic counterintelligence activity and foreign counterintelligence which created a "fundamentally flawed intelligence system."</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <b>Jennifer Sims and Burton Gerber (eds.)</b> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>Transforming Intelligence</p> 
                <p>Washington, DC: Georgetown University Press, 2005.</p> 
                <p>An academic and a intelligence professional present a very interesting collection of articles on various topics from the ethics to the operational problems associated with intelligence.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <b>Britt Snider</b> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p><a class="internal-link" title="The Agency and the Hill" href="/library/center-for-the-study-of-intelligence/csi-publications/books-and-monographs/agency-and-the-hill/index.html">The Agency and the Hill: CIA's Relations with Congress, 1946-2004.</a></p> 
                <p>Washington, D.C.: CIA Center for the Study of Intelligence, 2008.</p> 
                <p>A study of the CIA's relationship with Congress.</p> 
               </blockquote> 
               <b>Athan Theoharis, Richard Immerman, Loch Johnson, Kathryn Olmsted, and John Prados</b> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>The Central Intelligence Agency: Security under Scrutiny</p> 
                <p>Westport, CT: Greenwood Press, 2006</p> 
                <p>A look at the CIA since its inception, with entries on its leaders and more on the various successes and controversies that are part of its history.</p>

                <div class="" id="parent-fieldname-text-55a5766e45f049b42fce0fdbf970b9ae"> 
                 <p>The Office of Public Affairs (OPA) is the single point of contact for all inquiries about the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA).</p> 
                 <p>We read every letter, fax, or e-mail we receive, and we will convey your comments to CIA officials outside OPA as appropriate. However, with limited staff and resources, we simply cannot respond to all who write to us.</p> 
                 <hr class="centerline" styple="margin-top:2px;margin-bottom:2px;"> 
                 <h3>Contact Information</h3> 
                 <p><a href="#" class="contactTriggerFromLanding btn-lg btn-primary btn-block center-block" role="button">Submit questions or comments online</a> </p>
                 <p><b>By postal mail:</b><br> Central Intelligence Agency<br> Office of Public Affairs<br> Washington, D.C. 20505</p> 
                 <br> 
                 <p>Contact the <a href="/about-cia/privacy-and-civil-liberties/index.html" class="internal-link">Office of Privacy and Civil Liberties</a></p> 
                 <p>Contact the <a href="/offices-of-cia/inspector-general/index.html" class="internal-link">Office of Inspector General</a></p> 
                 <p>Contact the <a href="/contact-cia/employment-verification-office.html" class="internal-link">Employment Verification Office</a></p> 
                 <hr class="centerline" styple="margin-top:2px;margin-bottom:2px;"> 
                 <p><b>Before contacting us:</b></p> 
                 <p>Please check our <a href="/sitemap.html" title="Site Map"><b>site map</b></a>, <b>search</b> feature, or <b>our site navigation on the left </b> to locate the information you seek.                  <p>People from nearly every country share information with CIA, and new individuals contact us daily. If you have information you think might interest CIA due to our foreign intelligence collection mission, there are many ways to reach us. </p> 
                 <p>If you know of an <b>imminent threat</b> to a location inside the U.S., immediately contact your local law enforcement or <a href="https://www.fbi.gov/contact-us/field-offices"><b>FBI Field Office</b></a>. We will work to protect all information you provide, including your identity, and our interactions with you will be respectful and professional. Depending on what you provide, we may offer you compensation.</p> 
                 <h3>WHAT TO PROVIDE</h3> 
                 <p>If you feel it is safe, consider providing these details with your submission:</p>

                   <li> How you got the information you want to share with CIA</li> 
                   <li> How to contact you, including your home address and phone number</li> 
                  </ul> </span> 
                 <p>We cannot guarantee a response to every message. We reply first to messages of greater interest to us and to those with more detail. Our response will occur via a secure method.</p>
